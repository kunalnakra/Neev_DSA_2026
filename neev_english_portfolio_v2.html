About the Writer

About Me

My name is Neev Nakra and I am in Primary 6. I love reading — I read all kinds of books, from adventure and history to science fiction and mythology. Reading a lot has helped me become a better writer, because I pick up new words and ideas from everything I read. I started writing poems on my own because I find it fun and a bit of a challenge. None of the poems in this portfolio were school assignments — I wrote them because I wanted to. I like trying out different styles, like ballads and haiku, and I enjoy playing with words to make them sound just right.

Ballad of Edward Teach

Ballad

About this poem

I wrote this poem about Edward Teach, the real pirate known as Blackbeard. I wanted to try writing a ballad because it is a style that tells a story, and pirates felt like the perfect subject.

I tried to make the poem feel dark and exciting at the same time. Some of the words I used, like mizzenmast and figurehead, I had read in books about sailing ships. I ended the poem on purpose without a clear ending — because some stories are best left untold.

ballad form rhyme scheme historical subject

Read the poem

At the prow, in all its glory, the angel's figurehead stood, guarding the mighty ship, made out of fine oak wood.
The fearsome Jolly Roger, born aloft by the mizzenmast, flapped darkly in the wind, across a sea so very vast.
— · —
Lightning cracked open the sky, illuminating the hull, as we approached the island, of the broken open skull.
— · —
We trudged through filthy quagmire, to fulfil our heart's desire, seeking diamonds and gold, a story best left untold.
— · —
A rumble o'er the mountain, sent down my spine a chill, curiosity overtaking fear, we started to climb the hill.
— End —

The Tiger

Haiku Sequence

About this poem

This is a haiku sequence — three haiku that follow a tiger moving through the jungle. I liked the idea of using a repeating line, "Tiger made its way," like a heartbeat running through all three stanzas.

In haiku, you have to say a lot with very few words. I tried to show the tiger's movement and mood without describing it too directly — the reader can imagine the rest. The last stanza, where the tiger spots its prey, is meant to feel a little tense and quiet at the same time.

I made a deliberate choice not to capitalise the first word of each stanza. In traditional haiku, lowercase is sometimes used to reflect the continuous flow of nature — without the interruption that a capital letter brings. This was intentional, not a mistake.

haiku sequence nature writing refrain

Read the poem

through the undergrowth, softly and silently, the tiger made its way.
— · —
orange striped with black, traipsing through the jungle, the tiger made its way.
— · —
having found its prey, a few metres away, the tiger made its way.
— End —

Tribute to Tanjong Rhu

Published Poem
✦ This poem was chosen for publication on an external platform when I was 11.

About this poem

Tanjong Rhu is the neighbourhood I grew up in. I wanted to write about it because it means a lot to me, and I thought a poem would be more interesting than just describing it normally.

I used a repeated refrain — "Oh, I love Tanjong Rhu" — to give it a sing-song feel, like a song you might not be able to get out of your head. I also tried to include a little bit of its history, like the old shipyard, to make it more than just a personal poem.

I was really happy when it got published — it was the first time my writing appeared somewhere outside of school.
